# Summary of the Webinar (Bonterra)

#webinar #strategy #Ideas #projectIdeas

The webinar centers on how nonprofits should steward donors after the busy year‑end/holiday fundraising season, with an emphasis on *relationships over raw numbers*. While data and revenue targets matter, the speakers argue that long‑term fundraising success comes from:

- Understanding donor behavior and segmenting donors in meaningful ways.
- Avoiding two common pitfalls: going silent after campaigns and treating donors as ATMs.
- Building consistent, non‑ask touchpoints that show appreciation and impact.
- Shifting from transactional to transformational relationships grounded in trust, transparency, and mutuality.
- Using tools and automation (e.g., CRMs, email series, tagging, scoring) to make good stewardship possible even with limited capacity.
- Intentionally upgrading donors over time, especially first‑time, mid‑level, and major donors, by tracking engagement signals and asking at the right moment.

The first quarter after year‑end is framed as a key stewardship window: January is for thanks, February for engagement, March for highlighting impact and softly setting up the next campaign.

---

## Recommendations for Stewarding Post‑Holiday Donors

### 1. Shift Mindset: Relationships &gt; Revenue

- Measure success not only by dollars raised but by:
  - Donor retention and upgrade patterns.
  - Number and quality of relationships strengthened.
- See yourself not as “begging” for money but as:
  - Inviting donors to live their values.
  - Offering a way to create impact they don’t have time to create themselves.

---

### 2. Use Data and Segmentation Intentionally

Segment donors into meaningful groups and tailor outreach. Key segments mentioned:

- First‑time donors
- Recurring/monthly sustainers
- Major donors
- Lapsed vs. reactivated donors
- Peer‑to‑peer fundraisers
- Volunteers who also donate
- Event/hybrid event donors
- Anonymous donors (where you have their info vs. where you don’t)

Data points to track and review:

- New vs. returning donors
- Lapsed vs. reactivated donors
- Average gift size and upgrade patterns
- Channels that perform best (email, social, phone, in‑person, etc.)

Use segmentation to:

- Customize messages by segment needs (emotional acknowledgment, more information, greater transparency, etc.).
- Prioritize limited capacity (e.g., more 1:1 time for major donors, but still treat all donors with care).

---

### 3. Prioritize First‑Time Donors After the Holidays

- Treat first‑time donors as “full of possibility” and focus Q1 on:
  - Immediate, warm acknowledgments (email, phone, video).
  - Welcome packets and/or onboarding email series that:
    - Thank them.
    - Introduce your mission and programs.
    - Invite them to easy next steps (events, surveys, tours, volunteer opportunities).
- Use automation where possible:
  - Triggered welcome series for any new donor.
  - Automated follow‑ups (e.g., 3 days after gift, 2 weeks later, etc.).
- Goal: move them from a one‑time gift into an ongoing relationship and, over time, into other appropriate segments (e.g., recurring, mid‑level).

---

### 4. Build Non‑Ask Touchpoints (Especially in Q1)

Create regular, meaningful contacts *that do not ask for money* to avoid feeling transactional and donor‑fatiguing:

- Thank‑you communications:
  - Plain‑language thank‑you calls (30–60 seconds).
  - Personalized thank‑you videos (via platform tools or just your phone).
  - “Gratitude Tuesday” or similar thank‑you campaigns instead of a constant fundraising push.
- Impact storytelling:
  - Short impact snapshots: “Here are 3 ways your gift changed things since year‑end.”
  - Behind‑the‑scenes looks at programs, staff, and operations.
- Invitations to engage:
  - Surveys and polls.
  - Low‑lift events: coffee chats, site/farm tours, open houses, park meetups, volunteer days.
- Sample Q1 calendar:
  - January: all gratitude (thank‑you emails, calls, videos, impact stories).
  - February: invitations to engage (events, surveys, visits).
  - March: highlight the impact of Jan–Feb and softly preview upcoming spring fundraising (“We’ll be launching our spring campaign soon”).

---

### 5. Use Automation and Systems to Address Capacity Limits

For small teams or limited time, systematize stewardship:

- Build a 12‑touchpoint stewardship calendar:
  - Aim for 1 touch per month per donor segment, or 1 every other month if capacity is tight.
- Use CRM tools to:
  - Automate welcome and thank‑you sequences.
  - Schedule reminders for stewardship calls (e.g., “3 donor calls on the first Tuesday of each month”).
  - Tag, score, and group donors by segment and behavior.
- Use AI/writing tools to:
  - Draft emails, newsletters, and scripts, then edit for voice and accuracy.
- Create a **donor stewardship matrix**:
  - Rows: stewardship actions (thank‑you call, handwritten note, video, event invite, small gift, etc.).
  - Columns: donor bands (first‑time, monthly, mid‑level, major, etc.).
  - Define what each level receives automatically so it’s not reinvented each time.

---

### 6. Make Relationships Transformational, Not Transactional

Anchor your stewardship in:

- Mutuality:
  - Treat donors as partners, not just sources of funds.
  - Emphasize shared goals and community rather than hierarchy.
- Transparency:
  - Share numbers, outcomes, and challenges openly.
  - Offer clear explanations of where funds go.
- Consistency:
  - Contact donors regularly (not just at campaign time).
- Story sharing:
  - Use real stories of impact to make their giving feel concrete and meaningful.
- Invitations:
  - Invite donors to visit your programs, meet staff, join small group conversations, and offer ideas.
  - Use small group “vision brunches” or discussions with donors to co‑imagine the future.

---

### 7. Activate “Network Multipliers” (Peer‑to‑Peer & Ambassadors)

- Identify donors who:
  - Are enthusiastic and well‑connected.
  - Are willing to open doors, not just wallets.
- Ask them:
  - “Here’s our vision. Do you know anyone else who might be interested in helping us get there?”
- Encourage:
  - Peer‑to‑peer fundraising campaigns.
  - Ambassador roles at events or online.
  - Board and volunteer outreach to their networks.
- Use **matching gifts** strategically:
  - Ask major donors if their gift can serve as a match to mobilize others.
  - If a match goal isn’t fully met, transparently:
    - Celebrate what was achieved.
    - Discuss with the match donor about extending, converting the remainder for a later match, or closing it out.

---

### 8. Cultivate Trust and a Sense of Belonging

- Recognize that people fund leaders they trust:
  - Consider community surveys to assess current trust levels.
  - If trust is low, build a strategy to repair it before pushing for bigger gifts.
- Create belonging:
  - Ask: “What would need to happen for *me* to feel like I truly belonged to an organization?”
  - Offer:
    - Program updates tailored to interest.
    - Behind‑the‑scenes tours.
    - Personalized outreach from board/volunteers (which can feel especially meaningful because it’s not “staff doing their job”).
    - Small group donor conversations and feedback sessions.

---

### 9. Spot When Donors Are Ready for an Upgrade

Look for signals that a donor might be ready to give more:

- Engagement behaviors:
  - Opens and clicks your emails consistently.
  - Attends events or tours.
  - Gives more than once a year.
  - Responds to surveys.
  - Reaches out with questions or for more information.
- When you see these, transition to an upgrade ask by:
  - Opening with gratitude.
  - Reflecting impact: “Here’s what your last gift made possible.”
  - Sharing a compelling vision: “Here’s where we want to go next.”
  - Presenting a specific opportunity: “Would you consider increasing from X to Y to help us do Z?”

---

### 10. Don’t Forget “Small” Donors

- Treat $5–$10 donors with respect and gratitude:
  - They may be future major donors or “reclusive billionaires.”
- Offer them:
  - The same emotional acknowledgment.
  - Opportunities to deepen involvement (volunteer, advocate, attend events).
- Maintain the viewpoint that **every donor and every gift matters** to your community and your long‑term pipeline.
