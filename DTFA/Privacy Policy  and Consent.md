Yes, I’ve helped several nonprofits review their privacy policies and assist with making decisions about cookie notices and consent banners, and I can definitely share what we’re seeing across the sector.

**The short answer:**
Nonprofits are not always held to the same legal standards as for-profit companies, but the [privacy landscape is changing super fast](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=32bc1bd6-e7af-45df-932d-c49cfa20107c). More states are beginning to include nonprofits in their consumer privacy laws, and donor expectations around transparency have risen across the board (security breaches are no longer something that rarely happen). Because of that, most nonprofits are taking a somewhat **“proactive” rather than “bare minimum”** approach (budget allowing).

**On the policy itself:**
Right now DTFA’s privacy policy is a downloadable PDF, which isn’t ideal from an accessibility or transparency standpoint. A standard webpage version is best practice, and it makes it easier for users (and search engines) to understand how data is handled.

**On cookie banners:**
At this time (Nov 2025), [There is no U.S.](https://transcend.io/blog/cookie-banner-101#:~:text=What%20states%20require%20a%20cookie%20banner%3F) federal law that requires nonprofits to have a cookie pop-up, and most state laws use an opt-out model rather than opt-in. That’s why you’ll still see a mix across nonprofit sites some have banners, some don’t.

However, many organizations are choosing to implement some form of **cookie notice** ([important distinction)](https://secureprivacy.ai/blog/cmp-vs-cookie-banner) anyway because:

1. It simplifies compliance as more states add privacy requirements every year.

2. It builds trust with donors, who are now used to seeing these notices.

3. It prepares the org if they do ever have a security breach or get audited for data and privacy adherence

   1. Also, looking at DTFA’s metrics, not much EU/UK traffic, therefore no need to worry about GDPR - where opt-in is required.

For a U.S. focused nonprofit like DTFA, I would recommend a more “light-touch” approach for example, a small banner or footer link that explains cookies are used and links to the updated policy. If DTFA prefers a have stronger compliance or wants to expand its global audiences, a full consent-management banner (accept/reject for each category) would be the better choice.

However, it is really up to DTFA as an org on how they want to respond to these laws and requirements. We cannot give legal advice, I would recommend DTFA reading this [great resource that walks through orgs if they need one or not. ](https://transcend.io/blog/cookie-banner-101#:~:text=Do%20you%20need%20a%20cookie%20banner%20on%20your%20website%3F)I also highly recommend DTFA consult a lawyer who specializing in data and privacy law.

**Summary of what we recommend:**

- Convert the PDF privacy policy into an on-site webpage that’s easy to find in the footer. This would help immediately and a lot.

  - Examples:

  - <https://www.pih.org/pages/privacy-policy>

  - <https://www.redcross.org/privacy-policy.html>

  - <https://gfi.org/privacy-policy/>

- Add a simple cookie notice or small banner, or at minimum a “Cookie Settings” link in the footer.

- Audit forms and tracking tools (newsletters, contact forms, GA4, Meta Pixel, etc.) to ensure everything collected is disclosed in the policy.

- Keep an eye on new state laws so DTFA stays ahead of requirements instead of scrambling later.

There is a lot here, please take time reviewing and I’d be happy to jump on a call to talk through any questions.
